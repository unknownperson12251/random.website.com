09/03/25

She loves falling.
To drop without a reason to the bottom of the pit, then she touched her face bemusedly - a wet sensation - and saw red tainting her young fingers. She almost had to go for a stitch for her torn lip, but in the end, she left the injury as it was. Now, the scar was barely there.

Do you love falling, too?

The old narrative was getting rather boring. “I barely slept” “I have a headache” “My eye hurts” “Nobody understands me” “Nobody cares” “I’ll find reassurance alone in this despair”, repetitive, manufactured. Empty and soulless. With stories of other people plugged hastily into your ears, you concocted sob stories for your miserably uneventful life, reiterations, as if you were a broken record. Cowardly as you are, you yearned for some sympathy and love from your friends and teachers. Did you enjoy it when they babied you? Did you enjoy it when people gave you a modicum of their attention, fully directed, precisely only for you? Did you enjoy it?

Pathetic.

Sometimes, you crave for a feinting spell to hit. You pray to the higher figure up There, for a collapse down the stairs, on the train ride, in PE class - wherever, the location is not of particular interest to you. With your friends, you moan and whine about the tiniest details, with an “ugh”, an “oh my goodness, Jesus Christ”, about … how much homework you have. How much tasks you have on your fingers. Considering how you brought it onto yourself, even a part of you could recognise the ridiculousness of all of it.

That one day you stuttered 15 times, on your walk back home, the story resurfaced back again. One foot over the other, you thought: “Am I carrying things too far?” Your eyes darted to the calm ripples of the river, and the otters beneath the water. The midday sun looked at you, almost condescendingly with its brightness, enveloping the sky awkwardly in an almost grey blue colour. Squinting, you looked back down to the road, to see your skirt hanging loose on your hips. You readjusted the piece of fabric, dragged down by the weight of your phone and keys in its pockets. “Am I overthinking things too much? Or should I be more careful?” You forcefully looked ahead, slightly squinting to fight the Sun’s beaming rays of light. Fumbling with the pocket fabric, you gingerly withdrew your phone to change to a new song. “Am I laughing too much? Am I being annoying? Am I being too unhinged?” “Am I overthinking things again? Is this OK?”
Breathlessly laughing a bit to yourself, you continued on your journey back. “I’m sure I saw … I’m sure I felt their annoyance, really.” You were talking to no one in particular, but in spirit, she is with you and listening to your every word. Almost automatically, as though on cue, she pensively responded, “Do you think you should tone yourself down a bit?”
Left in deep thought, you slowed down your walking as your brain slowly tumbled into a halt. “What do you mean, tone myself down a bit? Is that even possible?” You conjured up a self-portrait with a skeptically raised eyebrow.
“You can consider … you can consider acting a little more mature than what you’re doing right now.” She seemed to look past you into some faraway distance.
“It’s their fault.” You mumbled to yourself, barely audible. “It’s their fault that I feel so safe being so expressive.” “Then stop,” she did not let you finish your sentence. “Stop associating with them.” You found yourself stopping at a crossroads. “Don’t let other people influence you and your image. Don’t let others jeopardise your reputation.” Seeing as the coast was clear, you ran for it. Your heart racing, you broke into a run as her words echoed in the back of your head. “Stop smiling if you have to.”

”I know I have depression,” you looked at her in frustration. “I know, don’t underestimate me.”
“Then why,” she looked straight into your darkened, restless eyes, “can’t you help yourself?” Under the dim white lights of your room, she seemed to be barely resemble a shadow, standing menacingly next to you, one hand on her hip. Relaxed to a chair in a room with no one, you played a little with your box cutter before you looked back at her.
“I can,” you looked at her with exasperated eyes. “It’s just that I …”
“Stop with the justifications,” her strong stare nailed you to an invisible wall. Her shadow consumed the room, a wave of energy, the elephant in the room - eating up the quiet and the peace. Half amusedly in response: “What am I supposed to say? That is the truth.” You cannot seem to meet her eyes. “Without all of this, I won’t be able to go on.” Your eyes diverted to the window. The sky had been dark for over an hour, a black velvet coating the sky, showered with tiny bright dots resembling stars. At the bright city life in front of you, you could only sigh.
“Stop victimising yourself.” Her words bluntly slapped your ears. “Stop pretending you are weak, stop asking for the help you know you don’t need. You know you can stand up on your own. You have to.”
Your lips tight, you glued your eyes to the bustling life outside - or rather, what remained of it. As the numerous dots of light moved on and about, you saw the road, empty and unoccupied, with single stalks of white light illuminating the peacefulness below. The trees stood static, there, their leaves unwavering, stubborn.
“What’s so wrong with wanting?” You finally turned back to face her.
“Greedy as you are,” she looked away from you, a hint of disapproval in her voice. “You know this would not work, and it will never work.” She walked to the door, staring straight into your eyes before she turned the doorknob. “Stop hoping.”
“Isn’t this what everyone wanted?” You raised your voice, for no particular reason. Your hands slowly curled into fists as you moved your chair with an ear-splitting slid and stood up. “For me to heal myself? For me to call for help and get what I need?” You took a stride, one, two, three, until you finally found yourself facing her, her blank face staring back at you with the same vigour it held earlier.
“Contradictory,” she leaned closer to your face, her pupils unmoving, her voice soft akin to a breeze. She suddenly smiled. “You are not helping yourself.” With her audacity, she slowly walked away, one foot after the other, soft and angelic steps.
“Can’t I hold onto something?” You found yourself screaming. With all the rage boiling in you, you ran after her figure. “Can’t I keep some identity to myself? Do I honestly have to lose every part of myself? Can’t you -” you grabbed her by the shoulder - “can’t you just,” you huffed another breath, “accept this as a part of me?” You pinned her to place. “This is me.” You pulled her face closer to your own. “You see me? This is me. This is my despair. Wouldn’t you want,” you found yourself slowing down, a senseless pit forming in your gut, “wouldn’t you want …” you felt something pushing out, your jaw firmly stuck in place. It took everything in you to keep it moving. “To help me a little? To save -” your voice wavered in how much strength it took to keep going - “me …?”
She let out a breathy laugh, a beautiful one, albeit mocking. “I wouldn’t want to help someone who couldn’t even help themself,” she weakly smiled. “It is shameful, how much attention you crave …” She leaned back a little. “As if you were begging people to look at you.” She laughed again. “How hard was it to stop smiling, really?” Her face contorted into something of mock curiosity. Disgust. “How hard was it to deny how everyone loves you now? How hard was it,” her finger found your chin, “living in denial like this?”
“But …” you found it harder to speak. “Didn’t you say I should hold myself back …? Isn’t this what I am … supposed to … do?”
“You hold yourself back to keep your image,” she stared straight through you, her pupils shrinking to that of a cat. “Not to construct a sob story of how hurt you are. Stop playing this game, as if you are so clever -” she gritted her teeth for the first time - “and calculated,” she took a little step towards you, so close that you could see the gleam in her eyes, “as if you know everything,” you felt a hard slap hit your cheekbone. Your entire body vibrated, your brain disoriented, and you could only look at her, her stone cold face of disgust, as she stared you down, pinned you to the ground, six feet underneath. “You narcissist.”

”Why didn’t you do anything?” Your mother’s hushed voice echoed in the darkness. In the pit, six feet underground, you could make out the shadows of your father, with the rusty, but sharp, kitchen knife in his hands, and your mother, who just sat up, her disoriented breathing striking heavy chords in the silence.
“Trust me, I wouldn’t kill her,” your father gritted his teeth, his arms half-heartedly moving upwards in surrender. “Trust me,” he hurriedly continued, “I wouldn’t find the heart to actually kill her,” he looked at the knife, before putting it down, “Really, I mean it when I say I couldn’t find the heart to end this with her.”
“It’s harder to kill than you’d think,” you thought. You were reminded of the days where you could only dream, of enough grit, to stab yourself or drown yourself in the nearby lake, to commit. It was hard to commit. It was hard to find enough justification for it.
“You know how close that thing was,” your mother tried to control her heavy breathing. “Why didn’t you even try to stop him?”
“I trusted him,” but you could not find the heart to say the sentence aloud. You stood there blankly, your eyes distancing themselves in dissociation, your soul seemed to leave your body. “It’s hard to commit to killing, I know that.” You could not speak. How would you explain yourself?
“Why didn’t you do anything?” Your mother’s voice pierced you again. Panic enveloped your figure as you could not find the will to open your mouth. Your body was stuck in place.
“Tell me.” Your mother raised her voice.
“...” You physically forced your mouth open. “I know it’s hard to kill.” You cannot say. “I trust him,” you decided to say. “I know he wouldn’t stab you.” You managed to muster.
“Say that again,” your mother’s voice was hushed. You could hear a sharp inhale coming from your infant sibling, sleeping peacefully. Both of your parents’ eyes seemed to pierce you, stab you into muteness. As much as you wish to disappear, you could not get out of this just yet.
“I don’t think he will.” You chose to say.
“What if he did?” Your mother raised her voice a little. Your sibling stirred in their sleep, and your anxiety stirred from its slumber. “What if he did?”
“ … I don’t know,” you mumbled. “I know he wouldn’t.”
“Why do you trust him so much?” Your mother’s stare pierced your gut. The uncomfortable monster sleeping inside reared its ugly head, and you could only feel the monstrous emptiness pulling you down six feet underground. “Why do you trust a potential killer so much?” Her voice screams provocative.
“Do not say that of me,” your father seemed to say. You could not register your surroundings anymore, only the darkness, the darkness, the darkness. “You can leave,” he turned to you, and the silence enveloping the lifelessness of the room. “Go to bed.”
“Don’t you leave me here,” your mother interjected, “don’t you leave me here with a murderer. Don’t you leave me here.”
You could only stand there like an NPC character.
“Don’t worry, I won’t,” your father attempted to reassure you. “Don’t worry about her, go to bed, it’s OK.”
